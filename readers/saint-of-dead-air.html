Chapter One — Feedback Check (revised for clarity)

Third-person limited — Jungwon

The arena isn’t a crowd yet. It’s a pressure: bass bleeding through concrete, a draft that smells like metal and lemon cleaner, a handful of early screams stitched into the air and swallowed by the rafters.

“Last pass,” the stage manager calls from the pit. Her headset clicks as she looks up at the grid. “Then dinner.”

Positions.

Jungwon steps onto the main deck—black paint scuffed to matte where flight cases have rubbed it smooth. White tape marks the geometry: center cross, corners, traffic lanes to the wings. Wedge monitors sit like dark teeth facing the mics. Upstage, the LED wall is asleep, a monolith of square scales.

He finds Solar in the left wing. Hat low. Mask tugged to her chin. She stands in the pocket where staff won’t hover, where she can watch without anyone fussing. Two fingers tap her throat—water—and he tilts his bottle in a small salute. Rituals steady rooms.

Click track in his in-ears: four bars of count-off, a metronome voice that doesn’t care who’s tired. He breathes with it. Leader math spools under the music: Sunghoon’s center of gravity (too forward, cue him later), Ni-Ki’s heel-toe timing (clean), Jay’s shoulder line (excellent). Keep all six in peripheral. Keep the room honest.

On seven, the air blinks.

It’s not the lights—it’s the reverb. The tail that should smear for a heartbeat after the snare is cut clean, like someone put a glass lid over the stage and shut it with two fingers. Jungwon’s heel lands on the center X. The lie slides across his ear: silky, flattering, wrong.

Rooms lie. He learned it in trainee studios with rattling vents and overconfident EQ—sound that rounds off edges so you stop testing it. When rooms lie, people get lazy. When people get lazy, bad tape lasts forever.

They hit post-chorus; the choreo snaps. From the wing, Solar smiles that private rehearsal smile she never performs in public, the one she lets him see—

—and her face shifts one degree left of real. Not broken. Optimized. Every plane softened to be less arguable. The smile holds but stops belonging to her.

“Hold,” he says. The word dies under stems and click.

He doesn’t shout again. He looks straight at the monitor stack and decides. It isn’t a button or a trick; it’s a choice, heavy enough to make every fader behave. The board hiccups. The wedges fold. For exactly seven seconds, all live mics carry nothing.

Silence is loud when you’re counting with your pulse.

One—two—three—four—five—six—seven—

A seam appears upstage, right where the painted flats meet. Not a stagehand door—those are dull gray with kick plates. This one is theatre-black and flush, the kind of door a set designer would brag about. It looks like it was always there. That’s how you know it wasn’t.

No one else flinches. Crew walk past with a coil of cable; Ni-Ki jogs his mark and rewinds like nothing happened; the world politely edits around the impossible so no one ruins it by noticing.

Jungwon crosses to the seam. Up close, it smells faintly of velvet and old gaff tape—the particular sweetness of fabric that’s absorbed a hundred shows. He pushes and steps into—

—an office. Or something cosplaying one.

Low ceiling. Black carpet. A counter like a reception desk married to a confession booth: waist-high, smooth, and a little too clean. Behind it stands a man in a velvet blazer the color of curtain time and a wireless headset he never touches.

“Welcome,” the man says, voice at the exact volume of a well-trained front-of-house. He checks a notepad with nothing written on it. “Seven seconds on the nose. Disciplined.”

Jungwon doesn’t answer that. “Where is she?”

The man’s eyes flick past Jungwon to the door he came through, where the stage is again a painted wall. “Intact,” he says pleasantly. “Separated from possession. It’s tidier.”

The words land like a label affixed to a person. Jungwon’s jaw works once. “I need her back.”

“Of course.” The man gestures down a short corridor. Three doors on the right, three on the left. Each has a handwritten label in white paint pen, neat as a merch table sign: BEST FOR BRAND. NOT A PRIORITY. TOO MUCH. A BETTER STORY. The doors… breathe. Hinges shift just enough to make the light in their seams pulse.

“What is this place?” Jungwon asks.

“The Dead Air,” the man says. “Seven seconds opens us. Seven seconds closes us. The world is too loud; people mishear themselves. We keep certain truths where they can be heard.”

“And you?”

He smiles in a way that never reaches his eyes. “Think of me as an usher. House rules, queues, the occasional reminder not to run.”

A voice leaks under the nearest door, tinny like an intercom that’s too proud of its own clarity: SHE’S BETTER AS A BRAND. SHE’S BETTER AS A BRAND. SHE’S BETTER—

The muscle between Jungwon’s brows tightens. “Open it.”

The Usher doesn’t move. “Your door,” he says, mildly. “Your decision. Count to seven when you open, count to seven when you close. And—do try not to overclaim.” His eyes tip upward. “The fixtures remember.”

Jungwon presses his thumb to his palm. Counting is easier when it has a ritual.

“One.”

The latch inside the door clicks, a shallow thnk like a mic muting properly. The seam brightens.

“Two. Three. Four. Five. Six—”

“Seven,” the Usher supplies, softly approving.

The door swings in on felted hinges.

The room is the size of a big dressing closet. It smells like hairspray, hot plastic, and the powdery cold of a portable steamer. A vanity mirror rings one wall, bulbs off, each one reflecting the others until the repetition makes distance feel strange. Mood boards are taped everywhere: printouts of Solar with different hair, different palettes, the same headline cut in bold across multiple slides.

On a small speaker above the mirror, the sentence loops with the flatness of a brand deck voice-over: SHE’S BETTER AS A BRAND.

Solar stands in the center of the floor. Perfect spine. Perfect shoulders. Perfectly present the way a cutout is present. Her eyes land on Jungwon and slide off like his face is glossy paper. She hums under her breath—a pitch that matches the room’s sentence exactly. The note is in tune. It just won’t belong to her.

“Don’t touch anything that writes,” the Usher says from the doorway, a maître d’ reminding a guest not to move the table settings. On the vanity, a stack of contracts shivers itself awake. The top one lifts from the pile. Two slender paper strips flex from the margin like legs. The page tastes the air—literal corners peeling—and steps toward Solar’s wrist with the prim confidence of something sure it will be signed.

Jungwon puts himself between Solar and the mirror. Not close enough to crowd. Close enough that the room has to choose which face to reflect.

He doesn’t reach for her. Rules feel like choreography he hasn’t been taught and somehow already knows: say the true thing; wait to be answered.

“Hi,” he says quietly. It lands like a weight on a rolling cart—stopping it without smash.

Her lashes lift. The loop hisses brand brand brand and tries to lay itself over his mouth.

“You’re my center when the room lies,” he says.

A thin black ring inks into the ceiling bulb above them, as if someone traced a perfect circle inside the glass. The light doesn’t flicker. The mark just… appears.

Solar’s brow pinches, the first imperfection in her face since he walked in. The loop stutters. The boards on the wall blur, as if a thumb smeared the toner while it was still warm.

The contract keeps walking. The paper legs tick against the vanity wood.

“Don’t,” Jungwon says, still not looking at it.

The page hesitates mid-step, offended, and folds its little legs back under itself to sulk.

Solar opens her mouth. No sound. Her throat works once, twice, like she’s testing whether her voice will obey her or the room. He watches the decision cross her face—the quick arithmetic of autonomy, of boundaries, of not being framed without consent.

“Say where ‘with you’ is,” she answers, hoarse and wholly hers.

The ring in the bulb thickens, one clean line becoming two, then settles. The loop loses a degree of certainty; on the mood boards, the headline fades a shade like sun-struck print.

Jungwon nods. This part should be simple enough to be true in any room. “Left wing,” he says. “Two steps back from the monitor stack. You hate the heat off the amps.”

Her mouth curves. Not the smooth, sellable curve from a stylist board—something uneven and alive. “I do.”

“Good,” he says, and the word clicks into place like a lock that was built to be turned exactly this way.

Behind them, the Usher smiles into his headset like he’s listening to a show only he can hear. “Lovely,” he murmurs. “Do mind your totals, Mr. Yang. The ceiling remembers.”

Jungwon glances up. One ring in one bulb. Manageable. A ledger he can carry for now.

He offers Solar his arm without touching. The offer is a shape in the air, nothing more. “We’re leaving this room,” he says—not an order, a map.

Solar nods and hums, and this time the pitch she chooses is a hair off the room’s phrase—the kind of off that makes harmony interesting. The walls tense, paper crinkling like a flinch. The door breathes open to the corridor again.

Outside, the handwritten labels glow a little duller, as if someone turned the saturation down. The Usher steps aside with practiced grace.

“Next,” he says, hands folded, velvet polite. “And remember: seven to close.”

Jungwon counts as he pulls the door in. “One, two, three, four, five, six, seven.” The latch catches. The seam goes dark. The ring above the vanity stays inked inside the glass, a halo on a bulb they’ll never turn on.

They walk without contact but perfectly in step. On the other side of the painted wall, crew move like a river around a stone. The world continues being practical. The corridor whispers with more doors, more labels that think they’re facts.

Jungwon resets his thumb to his palm. Counting is a metronome he can hold. The Usher watches like a teacher with a good student and worse news.

“One,” Jungwon breathes, already choosing to open the next room.


Micro Interlude — Interference (expanded clarity)

Third-person limited — Solar (120 words)

The note is obedient. That’s the worst of it. She can place it anywhere and it will sit perfectly, like a glass set down on a coaster. It just doesn’t turn when she calls its name.

The mirror throws back a face good enough to sell toothpaste. She knows that face. She hates it a little.

Jungwon steps into her line of sight and the room tips toward irritation—the kind she trusts. He doesn’t touch her. He doesn’t play leader with his hands. He says, “You’re my center when the room lies,” like he’s reading a code etched on the inside of his ribs.

A black ring inks itself into the bulb above them. The paper on the vanity gets ideas and grows legs.

“Say where ‘with you’ is,” she answers, because she can’t afford a man who claims her without a map.

He does. The door opens. The note in her throat finally looks at her, like a dog deciding who to sit beside.



Chapter Two — A Better Story

Third-person limited — Solar

The corridor is narrow enough that her shoulder could brush Jungwon’s if she let it. She doesn’t. The space feels like the backstage of a dream—black walls, low ceiling, and doors with tidy labels written in white paint pen, each in a hand too neat for anything real.

BEST FOR BRAND.
 NOT A PRIORITY.
 TOO MUCH.
 A BETTER STORY.

The air has the temperature of a theatre held between shows, and every door’s seam exhales—barely—like a person pretending not to breathe.

Solar’s voice sits in her throat like a borrowed ring. It fits. It gleams. It doesn’t belong to her.

She catches Jungwon watching the labels, not her. Leader eyes mapping exits, hazards, rhythm. His thumb rests against his palm as if he’s already counting, the oldest metronome in the world.

“What’s the cost?” she asks without looking at him. She needs the answer on the record, not softened in the quiet of his mouth.

The man in the velvet blazer, the one who named himself by how he stands—the Usher—answers as if they’re asking about a rental fee. “Every time Mr. Yang claims a truth inside a room and you answer it, a mark accrues on the fixture above. A neat tally. We believe in tidy infrastructure.” He lifts his chin toward the ceiling. “Too many rings, and the ceiling remembers. Things fall. People get hurt.”

“And if I claim?” Solar asks.

One corner of the Usher’s mouth lifts, polite and almost fond. “You may retune. If the lie is a pitch, you may set the key. It does not add ink. Most can’t hear the difference. You can.”

Because she is a singer. Because she lives inside her instrument. Because she has spent years finding the exact vowel that makes a note turn its head and follow her like a well-trained dog.

Solar keeps her eyes on the paint-pen letters. A Better Story looks the way a PR meeting sounds: patient, reasonable, hungry.

Jungwon steps closer to the door without touching it. “We open it,” he says. Not an order. A line on a map.

“We open it,” she repeats. The echo inside her mouth tastes right. “You count.”

He nods once. It’s not the yes he gives staff. It’s the yes he gives himself.

He holds his breath. Thumb presses palm.

“One.”

The latch inside the door clicks like a clean mute. Even the sound of it is inoffensive—no squeak, no complaint. Of course a room called A Better Story would have hinges that apologize for existing.

“Two. Three. Four. Five. Six—”

“Seven,” Solar says with him, because she likes how the word feels when they land it together.

The door swings inward.

The room is longer than the last, a rectangle with a seam of light running down the center like a runway. On the left: a wall of monitors, each playing a different edit of her life. On the right: a storyboard—actual pinned cards—headlines, B-roll suggestions, a column for “authentic moments” that looks like a grocery list written by a ghost.

The loop doesn’t talk this time. It narrates—a voice-over with a tone that belongs to a certain class of documentary: unbiased, which really means approved. The voice is female, mid-30s, friendly with an edge of knowingness. It tilts every fact into a plot arc.

“—and after months of relentless schedules,” the voice says, over footage of Solar laughing in a dressing room, “she finally learned to prioritize what really mattered: staying quiet, staying in line, staying grateful.”

On the monitors, Solar watches herself tilt a bottled water to her mouth and smile on cue. The camera pushes in. The smile arrives half a beat too early, then holds a beat too long. She knows that version of herself. It photographs well and shatters on touch.

The storyboard on the right offers options in black Sharpie:
 OPTION A: THE HUMBLE COMEBACK. (adversity → silence → triumphant return, no specifics)
 OPTION B: THE GRATEFUL GIRL. (thank fans, cry cleanly)
 OPTION C: THE ROMANCE REDIRECT. (deflect w/ cute ship moment; keep names vague)
 OPTION D: THE HARD WORKER. (montage of practice; no mention of cost)

At the bottom, in a thinner pen: OPTION E: THE VILLAIN. The E is ghosted, as if someone wrote it and then tried to press it back into the paper.

“Of course,” the narrator purrs as if secrets are a form of exfoliation, “the better story is the one people prefer to share.”

Contracts sit on a table under the monitors like place settings at a polite dinner. Their paper legs are already out, toes tapping.

Jungwon puts himself between her and the table without looking at it. His head turns once, cataloging. “What does it want from you?” he asks.

“To be easy to tell,” Solar says. Her voice arrives sounding like she hired it. She hears Jungwon hear it, too.

On the center monitor, a cut of her and Jungwon passes like a magician’s hand—two frames long, then gone. Solar’s stomach goes cold. She steps closer.

“Pause,” the narrator offers helpfully. The clip doesn’t pause. Better stories do not pause when asked; they crescendo.

Frame by frame, Solar watches a moment from a month ago: a backstage hallway, Jungwon at her shoulder, his hand hovering at her back without touching because he doesn’t take what isn’t offered. The story slot drops a gloss over the moment: secret romance? The caption suggests a date. The color grade warms skin, cools the world. It is careful, practiced, effective.

Solar’s mouth tastes like paper.

“I can break it,” Jungwon says. The tendon at his jaw flares. “If you want.”

The impulse in her muscles says yes. The part of her that has spent years learning where yes belongs says not like that.

“Wait,” Solar says. She steps until the seam of light down the floor beams under her shoes. The room lifts a degree, like a dog raising its head.

She listens. Not to the narrator. To the tone under it. The way certain rooms hum. Every rehearsal space in the company has its secret key—she learned that living inside headphones. The Dead Air rooms hum, too.

She hums a single pitch back. Not the same—a half-step above. The sound wavers in her own ears, beating against the room’s note in an ugly, honest friction. She shifts her vowel—all ee and no ah—and the beating steadies into a new grid.

The narrator stumbles. “—and after months of—” Breath. “—months of—” It sounds like a jogger who misjudged a curb.

The storyboard cards nearest her curl at the corners as if they’re too close to a heater.

Jungwon doesn’t move, doesn’t interject—just watches her like she’s solving a lock and he’s holding the hallway safe. That is the shape she will allow him to be: a gate that is hers.

Solar hums again, this time sliding up a clean whole step. She shapes the note on an oo, parting her lips less, lifting soft palate, finding the back-space that carries in a hall. The room’s hum tries to pull her down; she refuses and holds.

The contracts on the table shuffle their edges like unhappy crabs. The table wedges itself two centimeters away from her shin without moving.

The narrator tries one more time. “—the better story—”

Solar sours the vowel, flattening it, making it ugly on purpose. The voice cuts for a breath like a mic passing behind a speaker—feedback’s cousin.

She stops. The room steadies, waiting to see if she meant it.

“I don’t want a better story,” Solar says. She is not claiming. She is naming—the difference is in her ribs. “I want mine.”

The center monitor flickers. The hallway clip drains its gloss; the warm hue bleeds back to the fluorescent betrayal of backstage lighting. The look on Jungwon’s face is the one she remembers—unsellable because it isn’t for anyone else. Not possessive. Protective. A hand that guards the door so she can walk through it.

The contract nearest her foot hops, affronted, and tries to climb her ankle. Solar steps on it. A tiny eee noise comes out—like the squeak of a swing in late summer—and with it a smell she hasn’t thought about in years: wet asphalt and cheap popsicles and the way her mother’s car cooled when you opened the door.

She lifts her heel. The paper lies still, legs tucked, ink lines smudged as if it cried.

Jungwon’s voice comes low, careful not to put his shape over hers. “Do you need me to say it?”

Solar checks the ceiling. The fixture above them is blank glass. She likes it that way. “Tell me what you’d say,” she replies.

His eyes flick once to her—checking, not requesting. “We don’t sell this,” he says. “We don’t let them.”

She hears the shape of claim in it. She hears the question underneath: Do you want me to? Consent is not a formality, it’s a tuning fork.

“Not yet,” Solar says. “If I drift, catch me. But let me set it.”

A corner monitor, petulant, plays a version where she cries beautifully on cue. It is offensive the way a perfect lie is offensive. Solar hums again—this time she slides through the room’s key and lands on her own. The beat between their notes smooths into a low, satisfying tremble. The storyboard pins loosen, one by one, and the cards fall in a soft paper weather.

The narrator clears her throat as if embarrassed. “—the story—” she begins, and stops. No new story arrives to replace the one Solar just killed. The room waits. It does not know how to want without the voice.

Solar inhales. She doesn’t sing a lyric. She sings her name the way her grandmother used to say it when she woke her, different from stage introductions, different from office doors. Not quite the legal name. Closer to the kitchen table version.

The hum in the floor lines up. The seam of light through the room brightens—not more, exactly; truer.

Jungwon’s breath leaves his chest in a way only someone who knows him would hear. If he needs to say anything, he chooses not to. The ceiling above them remains clean glass.

“Seven to close,” the Usher reminds smoothly, as if she’s broken a rule by making a choice. He is not annoyed. He is keeping order.

Solar steps back to the threshold. Jungwon doesn’t reach for her. He doesn’t have to. She wants him to be where he is.

She nods once and lifts her hand—palm open, the beat they learned in the last room. Jungwon’s thumb returns to his palm.

“One,” he says, and she says it with him, together to seven. The latch clicks on a two that sounds like winter. On seven the door seals with a gentle conviction.

The label on the paint-pen plaque dims, as if someone turned a dimmer knob with two fingers. The words are still there. They simply don’t shout anymore.

They step back into the corridor. Solar’s throat feels… not normal. Not fixed. But familiar—like a room she hasn’t been in since it was repainted. Her voice shifts under her tongue, curious, like a dog coming when called and deciding to stay.

The Usher walks his hands along the counter as if smoothing tablecloth wrinkles. “Very good,” he says. He means tidy. “Most people enjoy Option A. You may congratulate yourselves on your restraint.”

Solar wants to ask him whether restraint is what he calls deciding not to be simplified. She doesn’t waste breath.

Jungwon’s eyes tip up. The light above the first room—the one with the brand boards—still carries its thin black ring like a mark on a wineglass. The fixture above A BETTER STORY is clear.

“Costs add up,” he says, mostly to himself.

“They do,” the Usher agrees, pleased with arithmetic. “Do avoid overclaiming.”

Solar looks at him. “What happens if he overclaims?”

The Usher’s attention turns to her as if she’s just let herself onto the guest list. “Fixtures fall,” he repeats in the tone of a man who enjoys precise outcomes. “Rooms collapse. We call it a reset.”

“And the people inside?”

“We prefer not to discuss blood on the carpet,” he says, gently apologetic.

“Then maybe don’t hang the lights so low,” Yunjin would say.

The thought of Yunjin arriving in a place like this—elbows sharp, mouth sharper—almost makes Solar laugh. The sound sticks in her teeth. She swallows it and looks at the next door.

TOO MUCH.

Jungwon follows her gaze. “You want to breathe before we open it?” he asks. He says breathe like a technician would say water—basic maintenance, not romance.

She tests a hum. The note turns its head. “If we wait, will the corridor try to choose for us?” she asks.

The Usher spreads his hands, all velvet. “We are not monsters, Miss Kim. We simply respect a schedule.”

Solar cuts a look at Jungwon. “We go,” she says.

He nods. Thumb to palm. They take their places by the door.

Before he counts, the corridor moves—only a sensation, a tilt, as if the stage above them has shifted load-in and someone has rolled a flight case over the seam. A ripple runs down the hallway.

The Usher’s headset clicks even though he’s not touching it. His eyes tip to where the painted wall meets the stage-that-isn’t. “Mm,” he says. “House left is getting interesting.”

Solar’s spine prickles. “What’s happening—up there?” she asks.

“Up there,” the Usher says, and the phrase is all theatre, no sky, “your colleagues are finding the first ledger mark.”

Upstage Left — The Real Arena
 Third-person limited — Yunjin

Yunjin spots it first because she is the sort of person who notices when something is just slightly wrong and then cannot stop noticing it.

They’ve run the last pass. The boys are grabbing water like pirates find barrels. Staff roll cable the way you coil the last of your nerves back into your body. Yunjin slips out of the wing where she has been illegally perched and watches the grid the way one watches a sky before rain.

“Do you see that?” she asks, elbowing Minji, who has drifted up like a quiet planet. “Tell me I’m not the only one going cross-eyed.”

Minji follows her finger. The metal above them is a cathedral of truss and fixtures; stare too long and it becomes math. One of the bulbs close to center—nothing special, a practical over the deck—has a ring traced inside the glass. Not a crack. Not a heat mark. A line like ink.

“It’s not soot,” Minji says, as if soot would be simpler. Her voice goes light the way it does when she’s trying to test how a room will treat her if she tells it the truth. “It looks… deliberate.”

“Who drew inside a light?” Yunjin demands. “With a Sharpie on a pole? Do we have pole Sharpies now?”

A tech with a coil over his shoulder passes and glances up. His gaze slides off the bulb as if the ring is a concept he has no room to store. “Looks fine,” he says, the way people say things are fine when they’d like to keep being paid.

Minji steps forward, tilts her head. For a second—just a second—something like a moth’s shadow drifts through the beam. She blinks and it’s gone. The skin at the back of her neck lifts anyway. “Hyein’s going to make a joke about aliens,” she says, to make the air behave.

Yunjin’s phone buzzes. She doesn’t look. The screen shows Solar and then hides itself. Yunjin sticks the phone back in her pocket and follows the heat she trusts: worry that tastes like metal.

“Where is she?” she asks.

Minji’s mouth presses flat. The answer wants to be here. It isn’t.

“She’ll walk back in any second,” Minji says. “She always does.”

“Uh-huh,” Yunjin says, which means I know you just lied to both of us. She looks back at the ring in the bulb and points, because pointing makes a thing real. “That wasn’t there an hour ago.”

Minji’s eyes soften, fixed on nothing above them. “No,” she agrees. “But I think it is now.”

They stand a minute longer like people at a crosswalk, sensing a car that should arrive and doesn’t. Then staff shoo them gently—space, space, always space—and the world continues to be practical. Yunjin lets it, because she has learned to bide her rebellions for when they count.

“Text me if you see her,” she tells Minji.

“I will,” Minji says, and means I am already listening for her the way you listen for your own name in a crowd.

They walk off in opposite directions. Neither one looks up again. The ring stays. The bulb doesn’t care. The grid remembers.

Solar can feel the echo of the room she just retuned in the bones of her jaw, a fine hum like the thinnest wire. Her name sits where she left it—closer to her mouth than it was an hour ago.

Jungwon waits by TOO MUCH with his hand not on the door. He isn’t fidgeting; he doesn’t do that. He is stillness aimed like a laser—watchful, patient, ready to be loud for exactly seven seconds and no more.

“Ready?” he asks.

“No,” Solar says honestly, and then: “Yes.”

The Usher looks pleased that the answers can both be true. “Seven to open,” he says, because rules are a kindness if you live by them.

Chapter Three — Too Much

Third-person limited — Jungwon

The corridor narrows the way hallways do in dreams—imperceptibly at first, then all at once when you notice you could touch both walls if you wanted. Jungwon doesn’t. He keeps his hands where rooms can see them, fingers relaxed, thumb resting against his palm the way it has since he was twelve and counting kept the world inside its lines.

The next plaque sits at eye level in neat paint-pen letters:

TOO MUCH

Solar studies it like sheet music she doesn’t trust. The Usher stands to one side smoothing invisible wrinkles along the counter, patient as a man taking tickets.

“Seven to open,” the Usher reminds, polite as a voicemail.

Jungwon meets Solar’s gaze. No permission asked, no order offered—just a rhythm check. She inclines her head a millimeter. He draws breath, presses thumb to palm, and sets the count.

“One.”
 A latch releases with the clean thnk of a mic muting correctly.
 “Two. Three. Four. Five. Six—”

“Seven,” Solar breathes with him, and the door swings inward.

It opens onto a corridor that forgot how long it was. Chalk lines stripe the floor at regular intervals—waist, bust, hem, ankle—like the room plans to measure them if they pass. Dress forms line the walls, canvas torsos with polished wooden arms, measuring tapes looped around their necks like leashes. Above, a shallow ceiling grid hums at the exact frequency of being shushed.

On both walls, thin LED bars wake and rise, marking the air like visualized audio. The bars settle green, then scold themselves down to a quieter green, as if the room has moderated its own voice and wants to be praised for it.

Midway down, a podium waits: plexiglass, on wheels, with a little red LIVE light. A narrow slot holds queue cards. One rises with a soft mechanical whirr.

I’m sorry for—

Solar’s mouth tightens. The apology wants to surface in the muscle memory where politeness lives. Jungwon steps—not a block, not a shield; he simply occupies the path the podium wants to roll.

“Keep your hands near your sides,” the Usher says from the threshold, like he’s announcing the soup course. “The mannequins dislike sudden gestures.”

The nearest dress form rotates its wooden head a quarter turn. Its measuring tape cinches one notch tighter with a ratchet-click.

The room’s hum is not a note so much as a posture: Lower it. Trim it. Take it down. The LED bars listen to their breathing and choose a number that seems acceptable to themselves.

Jungwon speaks first, close enough for Solar to hear and the meters not to panic. “I’ll set the perimeter,” he says, eyes on the ceiling grid. “You don’t have to fit inside it.”

The bulb directly above the podium inks a perfect black ring along its inner glass, as if a compass drew it. The grid responds by lowering a fraction: one breath’s worth, just enough that someone who watches roofs would notice.

Ledger, consequence. He files the math.

The podium offers a new card, eager to help:

I’m sorry my voice—

Seoul has little streets that feel like they never learned to be big. They pick one, shoulder to shoulder, masks up, baseball caps down, walking like civilian ghosts. A shop bell announces them with a chime shaped like a grandmother.

It smells like paper and good decisions. Behind glass: ink pads in a dozen reds, brass stamps, thin brushes that could ruin a life in the right hand. Solar’s eyes go starry; he files away the way her hands live in this place like they lived in rooms they had to retune.

“Looking for anything?” the owner asks, reading them as artists or trouble and deciding to be kind.

Solar taps the counter with a knuckle. “A small stamp,” she says, “for rests.”

The owner nods, pulls a tray from under the glass. Tiny brass heads, each cut to a symbol. She picks a quarter rest, laughs like a conspirator, and adds a little ink pad the exact shade of the red that’s been kissing Solar’s thumb for chapters.

“And this,” Solar says, impulsive, pointing at a spool of thin red thread.

Jungwon pays before she can argue because sometimes patriarchy is just efficiency and they’ve earned an easy fight. The owner wraps the goods in paper that crinkles like memory and bows as if she knows blessings when she sells them.

Outside, the air does the polite thing where it pretends not to be humid for ten minutes. They sit on a low wall and Solar stamps the pad of her own thumb with the new rest—tiny, precise. She lifts his hand with a question in her eyes.

He offers his palm. She stamps him too. The mark is small enough to be a secret, red enough to be true.

“Evidence,” he says, pleased.

“ERRATA,” she says, smug.

He threads the red string through his fingers and makes a quick, neat knot pattern—seven tiny bars spaced in a way only weirdos and lovers would clock. He ties it around her wrist. It’s practically nothing. It feels like a door that’s been told it can be a window.

“What’s this called?” she asks, eyeing the knots.

“Don’t Panic,” he says. “Version one.”

She kisses his cheek through her mask like a feral thief, which defeats the point and is perfect.

Afternoon — Han River, dumb sunlight
 Third-person limited — Solar

The river’s a field trip for adults. Bikers flirt with balance; couples on mats pretend they invented grass. They find a shady spot under the arm of a tree that survived construction and set up with a bottle of water, two rice balls, a strategic ignorance of time.

Jungwon lies on his back, forearm over his eyes, everything else sprawled like a manifesto. She flops down with her head on his shoulder because pillows are a scam when you live with a person.

“Always,” he says.

Night — the honest stair (again)
 Third-person limited — Solar

They steal seven seconds because superstition is really just gratitude in a dress. The stair smells like clean metal and all the kisses they didn’t get taxed for.

“Prize?” he asks, resting one palm beside her head, close enough to count as scaffolding.

She pretends to think, then hooks two fingers in his collar and pulls him down. It’s slow enough to be rude to anyone in a hurry. One. He smiles into it—two-three—like a man who takes notes on bliss. She deepens on four, thumb at his jaw, and the floor doesn’t tilt, hallelujah. Five-six belong to the part of her that learned to be greedy safely. They part on seven because rituals make good neighbors of wild things.

“Hydrate,” he says, helpless and adored.

“Shut up,” she says, glowing, and pulls a water bottle from behind her back like a magician.

They climb one flight back to her door hand-in-hand, pinkies hooked like children who learned the world and chose to stay sweet anyway. Inside, she kills the overhead and turns on the lamp with the shade that makes everything look like a decision.

“Come here,” she says.

He does.

They end the day tumbled on the couch—her cheek on his chest, his hand at her nape writing the count she doesn’t need but wants, again and again: one / two-three / four / five-six / seven. It’s not a door; it’s a cradle. It makes a home of her breathing.

Her phone buzzes one last time. KL: “FYI we got sign-off from the VP. The Ask Her Protocol is now… policy. Rest easy.”

Solar types rest = part of the music and turns the phone face-down forever, or at least for tonight.

“Say it,” he murmurs into her hairline, because he knows her and because words become truer when they’re said at the end.

“We’re out,” she says.

He smiles against her skin. “We’re home.”

She falls asleep to the metronome of him, that ridiculous steady. He stays awake long enough to commit this lamp-light to archive: the red rest on both their thumbs, the thread bracelet at her wrist, the breath that didn’t need rescuing, the world that, for once, didn’t ask for a receipt.

When he finally closes his eyes, the ceiling behaves. The night performs like a professional. No seams breathe. No rings ink. The only ledger that updates is the one under his hand, counting the seconds she trusts him with, balance due: tenderness.



Epilogue — The Seven We Keep

Mixed POV (montage → Solar & Jungwon)

They don’t go back to the corridor. They don’t have to.

But seams still remember themselves in little ways—like old habits that learned new manners.

At a community theater two cities over, a trainee freezes on an opening line. The house hushes in that dangerous way crowds do. Chaewon, guest-mentoring that night, lifts a hand and buys the kid a seven without calling it mercy. The audience breathes with her because the rest is part of the song.

In Tokyo, Kazuha holds a camera frame steady when a variety show tries a “messy reaction cam.” It stays tasteful because geometry wills it. Sunghoon’s edge-set draws an invisible box around a rookie’s boundaries. The broadcast looks better than it deserves.

Ni-Ki discovers a fake door in a shopping-mall pop-up, kicks it respectfully, and laughs when it prints 404: not a narrative. He sticks a 𝄽 sticker on the plexiglass anyway.

Minji mails a packet labeled ERRATA to a radio station that once snipped context out of a call-in. They run the correction on air. The host sounds like a man learning to enjoy vegetables.

Jake’s “Ask Her” badge set becomes a widget; producers drag it onto timelines like candy. Comms threads get cleaner. Fewer apologies age poorly.

Sakura stands at the edge of a rehearsal and the room behaves because it knows what a sentinel looks like. Sunoo teaches an intern how to warm a space that thinks it has to bite. The intern cries a little and then doesn’t.

Manager Seo prints slide decks with Seven Protocol in a font big enough to be religion. He talks to stagehands and gaffers and the kind kid in a headset who never thought policy could love him. Someone brings donuts. People sign their names.

Kim Lina sits on the quiet side of a legal call and says “no” five times and “ask her” six. When someone demands a window, she says, “She’s a person, not a push.” Later, she eats spicy ramen at a tiny table with bad stools. She pays and doesn’t call it penance.

The fandom does its feral, generous math. They trade rest stickers and ASK YOUR FRIEND beanies. Blue Hoodie takes seven seconds with her mom in the grocery store and gets a hug instead of a lecture. The clip goes minor-viral. The comments are better than the internet is supposed to be.

One night, quiet as a backstage prayer, Solar and Jungwon step onto an empty stage after a load-out. The rig is bare; the floor smells like wood and old thunder. Exit signs blink green like patient eyes.

No seam breathes. The silence here is earned.

Solar slips her hand into his hoodie pocket because that’s where it lives now. “Tempo?” she asks, smiling, because she loves asking even when she knows.

“Yours,” he says, and it lands like a door held open.

They don’t rehearse. They walk. Upstage to downstage, shoulder to shoulder, a slow line that makes the room look like a place invented for them instead of the other way around.

At center, she turns. He steps in. It’s small—no flourish, no low dip, no audience to feed. She touches his jaw with two fingers. He answers by cupping the nape he’s worshiped with professionalism and patience, thumb gentle, nothing to prove.

They count because it’s theirs: one / two-three / four / five-six / seven.

On and, she kisses him—quiet, terrible, right—and the building does not invoice them for joy. Somewhere a confetti cannon in storage chooses discretion. Up in the truss, nothing creeps. The grid holds like a friend who learned how.

“Hydrate,” he breathes, helpless, beloved.

“Shut up,” she says, happy, and presses her forehead to his—home, punctuated.

She pulls a tiny brass stamp from her pocket—the quarter rest they bought in the stationer’s alley—and smudges a red 𝄽 on the backside of a long-forgotten cable trunk where only the bored or the blessed will ever see it.

“What’s that for?” he asks, amused.

“Proof,” she says. “Rests happened here.”

He looks at her wrist where the thin red thread is still knotted into Don’t Panic (v1). He threads their fingers, carding through the bracelet like it’s a talisman he trusts. “Show me v2 later,” he says, shameless.

“Rude,” she says. “Correct.”

They wander the wings. In the ghost light, a laminated card sits on a music stand—THE ASK HER PROTOCOL—print crisp, edges dinged by hands that actually used it. Someone has doodled a tiny sakura blossom next to Audit Trail. Someone else added ramen clause in blue pen with three exclamation points.

“Family,” Solar says, because it’s the only word big enough and small enough.

“Gate,” Jungwon says, because that’s what he chose and keeps choosing. “Not owner.”

Back in the center, she folds into his arms like a decision. He holds like a promise that knows where it ends: around her, not on her. They don’t rush. Seven belongs to them; they spend it lavishly, then let it earn interest in the parts of the day that don’t need noise.

When they step off into the hallway, the world starts up again—gurneys rolling, someone laughing at a text, a vending machine pretending water is personality. The only caption is the one they chose.

Outside, the night is doing its job. Inside, they leave the door how they found it: closed, clean, asking.

Later, there will be tour buses and studio days and fights about dishes that end in kisses because ritual is the only magic anybody ever proved. There will be trainees who learn to keep seven seconds in their pockets, and executives who sign their actual names, and fans who discover that rest doesn’t mean cowardice; it means music.

And there will be two ridiculous people—in stairwells, on rooftops, in kitchens with villain stoves—counting for each other, tender as math.

We’re out, Solar will say, sometimes.

We’re home, Jungwon will answer.

One / two-three / four / five-six / seven.

The door listens. The world learns. The rest holds.
