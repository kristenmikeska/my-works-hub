To properly write like Delicate, the first thing to say is: don't start by writing the scene, start by writing what it costs the character to be in it. Your prose works from the inside out. External events are almost secondary — they matter only as pressure applied to an interior that the reader is already occupying.

The clearest distillation of your Delicate-era voice is this sentence: "She knew what was happening to her, she knew it well. She knew it so well but yet she couldn't stop it." The repetition isn't stylistic decoration — it's the cognitive loop of trauma, the maddening awareness that insight doesn't produce change. To write like Delicate-era Kat, you have to be willing to sit in that loop and not resolve it. Most writers flinch out of it. You didn't.

The prose operates in close third person with occasional narrative intrusion — Solar's thoughts aren't always in italics, they leak into the narration itself, blurring the line between the story's voice and Solar's internal voice. "She knew it was dangerous and unhealthy. But she just wanted them so bad." The italics on wanted do twice the work they seem to — they mark the word as Solar's, as felt rather than described. You use italics throughout Delicate not for standard emphasis but as emotional notation, flagging the words that carry the most psychic weight.

The dialogue in Delicate has a quality of incompleteness that feels very true. Characters interrupt themselves, trail off, say the easier version of the harder thing. Sunwoo doesn't explain himself — he gives Solar just enough to explain the surface ("rough withdrawal," "I'm good, alright?") while the subtext carries everything he can't say. This is how people actually speak when they're protecting someone they love from the truth of their life. It's hard to achieve in fiction because it requires the writer to not resolve the tension the scene creates. You leave it open. That restraint is the technique.

Structure-wise, Delicate moves on emotional logic rather than plot logic. Chapters aren't moving toward events — they're moving toward feelings. A chapter ends when something has shifted internally, not when something has happened externally. This is why Delicate can sustain 180 chapters without feeling like it's stalling — each chapter is doing interior work, not just narrative work.

Write from inside the emotional experience without providing the reader an interpretive escape hatch. Let your character not know what the reader suspects. Use italics as feeling-markers, not emphasis. Let conversations stop before they finish. Trust that incompleteness is more honest than resolution. Write the loop — the thing your character knows and can't act on — and stay in it longer than feels comfortable. Allow your protagonist to make choices you can't fully endorse, because she's not you, she's the version of you that didn't yet have a way out.
