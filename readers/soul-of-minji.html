soul of minji

A crash, a missing Minji, and a mission that behaves like a game objective.

Act I – The Spark

The practice room smelled faintly of hairspray and sweat, the mirrors still fogged from rehearsal. Solar leaned against the wall, nursing a half-empty water bottle, watching Minji laugh at something Yunjin said. The sound bubbled up light and bright, and Solar felt her lips tug into a smile before she could stop herself.

Minji caught her watching. Just for a second. Her head tilted, a strand of hair slipping loose from behind her ear. And then—soft as a secret—she winked.

Solar nearly choked on her water.

It had been like this for weeks. Not dating, not anything that needed to be labeled, but something threaded between them anyway. A brush of fingertips when Minji handed over a towel. A shared look across the cafeteria table that lasted too long. That low hum of tension buzzing in Solar’s chest like static electricity.

Yunjin noticed, of course. Yunjin always noticed.

“Girl,” she drawled one evening, plopping down beside Solar, “if you don’t do something about this, I might.”

Solar shoved her shoulder, laughing, cheeks warm. “Shut up.”

“I’m serious!” Yunjin grinned, eyes sparkling mischievously. “The way you two look at each other? Embarrassing. Peak teen drama levels.”

As if on cue, Minji wandered over, hair still damp from a shower, hoodie swallowing her frame. She flopped onto the floor across from them, tugging at the strings of her hood.

“Hey,” she said softly, eyes flicking to Solar’s before darting away. “You tired?”

Solar felt the question land differently. Like it wasn’t just about practice. Like Minji could see past the sweat and exhaustion, straight into the quiet ache beneath her skin.

“I’ll survive,” Solar teased, smiling.

Minji’s lips curved. The kind of smile that stayed with you, even after she left the room.

Yunjin groaned loudly, throwing her head back. “Ugh, you two are killing me. Just confess already.”

Both Solar and Minji flushed, fumbling excuses at the same time. The moment passed in a tangle of laughter and too-fast heartbeats, but Solar carried it with her the rest of the night. That almost. That maybe.

It was the beginning of something—whatever it was.


Act II – The Crash

It was late, and the city lights blurred against the car window like smeared paint. Solar sat curled up on the couch in the dorm, scrolling through her phone absentmindedly, when a notification buzzed across her screen. A message from Yunjin:

Going for a drive with Minji. Don’t wait up 💖

Solar rolled her eyes. Classic Yunjin. She was always dragging people into little adventures, sometimes to cafés at odd hours, sometimes to random scenic spots she claimed had “life-changing vibes.” But tonight… Solar wished she’d gone with them. She could already picture Minji in the passenger seat, hoodie strings pulled tight around her face, soft laughter filling the car. The thought made her heart squeeze with something sharp and sweet all at once.

She typed a quick reply—something teasing, something about how they better bring her back snacks—but before she could hit send, her phone buzzed again.

This time, it wasn’t a message.

It was a call. Unknown number.

Solar frowned, thumb hesitating before answering.
 “Hello?”

The voice on the other end was garbled, static crackling through like a bad radio station. All she caught were fragments: “…accident… crash… come now…”

Her chest went cold.

By the time she grabbed her jacket and bolted for the door, she was shaking so hard she nearly dropped her phone.


The drive to the location felt endless. The whole time, Solar’s mind replayed every interaction with Minji, every smile, every almost-touch, every moment she hadn’t dared push further. And now what? What if she never got another chance?

When she arrived, she saw it: the twisted wreckage of Yunjin’s car, headlights still glowing faintly, a jagged skeleton in the middle of the road. Steam rose into the night like smoke from a dying fire. But no one was there. No Yunjin. No Minji.

Just silence.

Solar’s breath came fast and shallow. She stumbled closer, her sneakers crunching over broken glass. The sight of Minji’s hoodie caught in the shattered door made her stomach lurch.

She fumbled her phone out of her pocket, dialing Yunjin with trembling fingers.
 “Pick up, pick up, please—”

Finally, a click.
 “Yo,” Yunjin’s voice answered, hoarse but calm.

Solar nearly screamed. “Yunjin?! What the hell happened?! Where are you?!”

A pause. Then Yunjin sighed like she’d just run a marathon.
 “I’m fine. Few scratches. But you need to come here.”

“Where’s Minji?”

Another pause. Too long.
 “She’s… she’s with me. Just come.”


When Solar finally found them, it wasn’t where she expected. Not a hospital. Not the side of the road.

It was some unfamiliar building on the outskirts of the city, fluorescent lights buzzing faintly above the entrance. Yunjin leaned against the wall outside, a bandage slapped carelessly over her forehead, dried blood staining the collar of her shirt. She grinned when she saw Solar, like nothing could faze her.

“You took your time,” she teased weakly.

Solar’s heart almost burst. She ran forward, grabbing Yunjin’s shoulders. “Don’t joke! I thought—you could’ve—” Her voice broke.

Yunjin’s grin softened. She patted Solar’s arm. “Relax, girly pop. I’m tougher than I look.”

Solar pulled back, her voice sharp. “Where’s Minji?”

Yunjin’s expression shifted then. Something unreadable. She lifted a hand, pointing at a closed door down the hall.
 “She’s in there. Hasn’t come out since.”

Solar blinked, her pulse roaring in her ears. “What do you mean? Is she hurt? Why is she alone?”

Yunjin’s gaze softened again, almost… sympathetic. “Trust me. She’ll want to see you. Go.”

Solar froze. Her instincts screamed not to. Minji must be shaken up, traumatized—she didn’t want to barge in. But the way Yunjin said it, quiet and firm, left no room for argument.

Swallowing hard, Solar walked toward the door.

Her hand hovered over the knob, trembling. She raised it and knocked gently.
 “Minji? It’s me.”

There was a pause. Then, faintly:
 “…Come in.”

Solar exhaled, bracing herself, and pushed the door open.

And what she saw made her blood run cold.

It wasn’t Minji.




Act III – The Door

The fluorescent lights buzzed faintly above Solar’s head as she stepped into the hallway. The world felt… wrong. Like the air itself held its breath. Her shoes echoed against the tile, each step dragging her closer to the closed door.

Her knuckles brushed against the wood. Just a light knock.
 “Minji?” Her voice cracked. “It’s me.”

A pause. Then, faint, muffled words seeped through:
 “…Come in.”

Solar exhaled shakily, twisting the knob. The door creaked open.

She expected Minji. Expected a fragile smile, bruises maybe, tears she’d gently wipe away.

But the figure in the room wasn’t Minji at all.

“Cherry?” Solar froze in the doorway.

Her best friend looked up from where she sat cross-legged on the floor, hoodie hood pulled tight, a strange stillness in her gaze. The familiarity hit Solar like a slap—but it was wrong. Cherry’s posture was too calm. Her eyes too knowing.

“What the hell are you doing here?” Solar demanded, voice rising with panic.

Cherry tilted her head, a small, eerie smile tugging at her lips.
 “I saved her life,” she said softly.

Solar blinked. Her throat closed. “Wh—who?”

“The girl you’re looking for.” Cherry’s voice wavered like it wasn’t entirely hers. Her hand pressed against her chest. “Minji.”

The room seemed to lurch sideways. Solar staggered a step back.
 “…What are you talking about?”

Before Cherry could answer, a second figure moved in the corner.

Solar’s stomach dropped.

Another Cherry.

Sitting casually on a chair like she’d been there the whole time, sipping from a Dutch Bros cup, straw between her lips. This one grinned lazily, like the whole situation was some kind of inside joke.

Two Cherrys. Identical. Breathing. Staring at her.

Solar’s pulse screamed in her ears. “What the—no, no, this isn’t—”

The Cherry on the floor—Minji-Cherry—lifted her gaze.
 “They transferred my soul,” she whispered. “I’m Minji. I’m right here.”

The world tilted.

Solar stumbled against the wall, fingers scraping the cold paint. Her chest ached, her brain trying to fold in on itself.

“You’re lying,” she said, breathless. “This—this is insane. Minji wouldn’t—”

The other Cherry cut her off, laughing, voice distorted for half a second, glitching like a broken record.
 “She’s telling the truth.”

Solar’s hands shook. She wanted to run, but her legs rooted to the floor. Minji’s hoodie lay crumpled on the chair beside Cherry, the same hoodie Solar had seen at the wreck.

This wasn’t a coincidence.

Solar’s eyes darted back to Minji-Cherry. “If you’re her… if you’re really Minji… then—prove it.”

For a moment, silence. Then Cherry’s lips parted, voice trembling:
 “That night after rehearsal… you told me you’d survive. I smiled, but I wanted to say, ‘Don’t just survive. Live with me.’”

Solar’s breath caught.

She remembered. Minji’s smile. The unsaid words hanging heavy in the air.

Her vision blurred. Her best friend’s face… but Minji’s words. Minji’s soul.

Solar’s knees gave out, and she sank to the floor, hands in her hair. “No. No, no, no…”

Yunjin appeared suddenly in the doorway, her silhouette sharp against the flickering light. She didn’t look shocked. If anything, her expression was grimly resigned.

“Told you she’d want to see you,” Yunjin murmured.

Solar whipped around, tears stinging her eyes. “What the hell is going on?”

Yunjin’s jaw clenched. Her gaze flicked between the two Cherrys. “I don’t know all of it. But what I do know?” She nodded toward Minji-Cherry. “That’s her. That’s Minji. And you’re the only one who can fix this.”

Solar’s breath caught. Her skin prickled with the weight of something bigger than herself.

Minji’s soul, trapped in Cherry’s body.

And Solar—the one chosen to bring her back.

The air grew heavy. The lights buzzed louder, flickering. Shadows stretched unnaturally long across the floor, crawling toward her feet like fingers.

The world was unraveling.

And Solar? She was about to be thrust into the mission of her life.



Act IV — The Mission

The door shut behind Solar with a soft click—too gentle for how violently the world shifted. The hall, the buzzing lights, the antiseptic smell of the building: all of it folded inward like paper, edges kissing, creases sharpening. The floor collapsed into a grid of luminous tiles suspended over dark water. The air tasted like copper and thunder.

Yunjin stepped in beside her, a hand grazing Solar’s elbow. “Okay, so, just to check—we’ve officially left normal. You good?”

Solar couldn’t find her voice. The sigil on her palm—she hadn’t noticed it until now—burned into visibility: a thin gold line shaped like a crescent over a small square. It pulsed once, twice, in time with her heartbeat.

Across from them, Minji-Cherry stood quietly, one palm over her borrowed chest, eyes too bright in Cherry’s face. The other Cherry sat on a chair that somehow hadn’t been swallowed by the new geometry of this place. She sipped from her cup, unfazed. Two lives in parallel. One soul displaced.

Solar swallowed. “Tell me what to do.”

Before anyone could answer, a line of text glitched into the air between them, like a HUD only Solar could see:

[System] Objective added: Return Minji to her body.
 Route: The Unclaimed Corridor → The Archivist’s Gallery → The River of Almost → The Mirror-City → The Sanctum of Names.
 Note: The chosen must bear the cost.

Yunjin squinted at nothing. “Is there… text in the air? Be honest. Don’t gaslight me.”

Solar let out a shaky laugh. “You don’t see it?”

“Love that for you,” Yunjin said, then rolled her shoulders. “Alright, speedrun strats. I’m your healer-slash-bard. If I start singing, pretend it’s a buff.”

Solar flexed her marked palm. The sigil warmed.

“Go,” Minji-Cherry whispered, voice not-quite-Cherry’s and yet soft like Minji’s, a sound that steadied Solar. “I’ll be close. I always am.”

The walkway unfurled like a tongue of light. Solar stepped forward.

1) The Unclaimed Corridor

At first it seemed endless: glowing tiles humming beneath her shoes, the darkness beneath like an ocean. Solar could swear she heard distant chatter—voices layered on voices, all familiar. Her own, Minji’s, Yunjin’s, a thousand versions of conversations that never happened.

“Don’t listen too hard,” Yunjin warned, jogging to keep pace. “This place loves to lure you with ‘what ifs’ and ‘should haves.’ That’s bait. We don’t take bait.”

Something flickered at the edge of Solar’s vision. A door appeared on the left, lacquered black, with an eye-shaped keyhole and no handle. Another appeared on the right, this one a pale wooden arch with wind chimes made of pressed glass.

“Which one?” Solar asked.

“Neither,” Yunjin said cheerfully. “If the level wants you to pick, you probably don’t.”

A third door formed directly ahead: rough stone, heavy, scarred. The sigil on Solar’s palm throbbed, answering it—the way a compass needle turns.

She laid her palm to the stone. It was cold, ancient. The mark flared, heat surging up her arm. Metal groaned. The door slid aside.

A rush of scent—dust, old threads, the dry breath of archives—washed over them.

Yunjin peered in. “Oh wow. Library vibes. Do we get a tutorial boss, or are we going straight to a lore dump?”

2) The Archivist’s Gallery

They stepped into a hall of frames hung on cables, floating at various heights like stars. Each frame held a memory, but they weren’t crisp images—they blurred and shimmered if stared at too long. Solar recognized flashes: Minji’s wrist with a woven bracelet; a vending machine glow; the crease of Minji’s smile the first time she’d said hello for real, not for show.

A figure drifted out from behind a frame. Not walking—gliding, robes stitched from index cards and ticket stubs, face hidden behind a stack of postcards fanned like a mask.

“Welcome, Chosen,” the figure said, voice papery. “I am the Archivist of Almost. Here is what was nearly yours.”

Solar’s pulse skipped. “I’m not here for almost. I’m here for Minji.”

“Then you must sort the true from the pretty,” the Archivist replied. “Three frames. Choose the ones that belong to her and you together. Reject the one that flatters your fear.”

The frames shifted until only three hovered before Solar:

  • In the first, Solar and Minji sat at a café table, pinky fingers linked under the tablecloth, faces hidden. Safe. Secret.
  • In the second, Solar watched Minji from across a dance studio, both of them exhausted, neither of them brave enough to bridge the gap. Tension hummed like a wire.
  • In the third, Minji pressed a paper crane into Solar’s palm in a crowded hallway, eyes fierce. No hiding. No almost. A start.

Yunjin leaned in, voice low. “Feels like a trick question.”

Solar knew it, bone-deep. “The first is a wish. The second is how we stayed. The third is…” Her throat tightened. “…what we were about to choose.”

The Archivist’s mask tilted. “Then refuse the softness that keeps you small.”

Solar lifted her hand. The sigil’s light poured into the first frame. It fizzed, burned, and then became volatile—melting like sugar. The frame shattered into confetti. The other two steadied, their images sharpening: studio mirrors, hallway crush, paper crane, that look that tasted like yes.

“Accepted,” the Archivist intoned. They reached into their robe and pulled out a thread—thin, gold, warm. “A binding of what’s true. You will need this where names are asked for and stolen.”

Solar realized then that the gold thread matched the curve in her palm sigil. She took it gently and wound it around her wrist. It disappeared into her skin, leaving warmth.

“Thanks, librarian boss,” Yunjin chirped. “No late fees, right?”

The Archivist bowed. Their postcards fluttered like moths. “Beware the River. It drowns more in maybe than in water.”

3) The River of Almost

The gallery opened onto a riverwalk—if a riverwalk had no shore. Water hung in the air like folded silk, liquid suspended in mid-curve, waves frozen in the moment before breaking. The surface reflected scenes like windows. At every step, the water tried to offer Solar something: a do-over, a detour, a pain-free shortcut.

“You want to look,” Yunjin murmured. “But don’t.”

Solar couldn’t help it. One surface showed a version of her where Minji never got in the car. Another showed them as friends forever, safe and platonic, free of risk. Another showed Minji laughing in someone else’s arms—Solar absent, free of yearning, free of everything.

The fourth—this one grabbed Solar by the ribs—showed Solar and Minji in a small kitchen somewhere, yawning, making coffee in worn socks, shoulders brushing, rings on their fingers. Domestic. Simple. A future that didn’t itch.

The river pulsed, hungry. Take this one, it breathed. Skip the hard. Pick the ending now.

Solar’s chest hurt. She reached a hand toward the vision without meaning to.

“Hey.” Yunjin’s hand closed around Solar’s wrist. Gentle. Steady. “We don’t get the epilogue before the plot, babe.”

Solar dragged her gaze away. The sigil in her palm burned; the gold thread at her wrist thrummed like a plucked string.

“Okay,” Solar said, voice hoarse. “Okay. I’m not here to steal a shortcut. I’m here to walk.”

The river hissed, disappointed. The frozen waves shivered. A narrow bridge – impossibly thin, impossibly solid – unfolded across the water.

Halfway across, something rose—the shape of a girl wearing a smile with too many teeth. Her hair floated as if underwater. Her eyes were doll-bright, empty and watching.

“Danielle?” Solar asked before she could stop herself. The name came out as both question and warning.

The girl’s grin sharpened. “Not quite,” she sang. “Just a warden made of every jealous glance and every hungry feeling. I keep what gets borrowed. I keep what gets lost.”

She held up a glass orb. Inside it, something darted—like a firefly, like a heartbeat—a flicker that called to Solar’s bones. Minji. A shard of her, captured.

Yunjin stepped forward, body angled like a shield. “Alright, river goblin—”

“Warden,” the not-Danielle purred.

“—whatever,” Yunjin went on. “She’s with me. And we’re taking our soul back.”

The warden cocked her head. “Are you? Costs apply.”

Solar lifted her marked hand. It glowed brighter, spilling warm light. “Name your cost,” she said, though the words scraped her throat.

The warden’s smile glitched. “The cost is always the same: trade an old defense for a new promise. Lose one mask. Speak one truth out loud.”

Solar’s stomach dropped. She had so many defenses they were practically wall insulation. Which one? The one where she laughed things off? The one where she acted fine while sinking?

“Pick fast,” Yunjin murmured, eyes scanning for an opening. “Her health bar looks low-key infinite.”

Solar’s lungs trembled. “Fine,” she said, more to herself than anyone else. “Take the lie that I’m safer in ‘almost.’ Take it all.”

The bridge shook. The river groaned. The warden flinched as if struck. The orb in her hand cracked down the middle; leaked light bled through her fingers.

She snarled, teeth lengthening, pretty surface tearing to reveal the jank beneath. “You think one truth buys you everything?”

“No,” Solar said, voice growing steadier. “But it buys me this.”

She surged forward, sigil blazing. Yunjin moved with her, a blur—no weapon in her hands, only a voice. She started to sing, low and fierce, a melody Solar had heard a hundred times in practice rooms: not showy, not polished for an audience, just a line of sound meant to keep someone going. Buff aura: +Courage. +Clarity. +Dumb bravery.

The warden lashed out with water that wasn’t wet—sheets of longing that tried to fold Solar back into the river, back into someday. Solar cut through it with the gold thread woven through her skin, each slash leaving a clean line of sunlight.

“Return her,” Solar demanded.

“Make me,” the warden hissed, but her edges were fuzzing, performance glitching.

Solar reached the orb and pressed her sigil to it. Light burst between her fingers, a flare throwing Yunjin’s shadow long and sharp across the bridge. The orb shattered. The tiny light shot into Solar’s chest like a bird returning to its nest.

For an instant, she heard Minji—clear, impossibly close: I’m here. Keep going.

The warden’s body fissured like cracked porcelain. She gave Solar one final, hate-soft smile…and dissolved into river spray.

The bridge steadied. The frozen water thawed just enough for the waves to turn and go, like something exhaling for the first time in years.

“Okay,” Yunjin panted, hands on her knees. “Boss one down. Loot drop?”

At Solar’s feet: a small, folded paper crane. Yellow. Warm to the touch. She tucked it carefully into her pocket.

[System] Shard reclaimed: 1/3.
 Next: The Mirror-City.

“Of course there’s more,” Yunjin sighed. “Wouldn’t be a proper arc otherwise.”

4) The Mirror-City

They stepped into streets paved with broken mirror—endless, reflecting the sky, the buildings, and them. Every pane showed a different version: Solar scowling, Solar running, Solar lying down and refusing to move. Minji in a dozen outfits, a dozen moods, none of them the one Solar needed right now.

At the city’s center stood two figures. Both were Cherry. Both wore Cherry’s favorite hoodie. Both smiled, small and tired.

“Solar,” said the one on the left, voice soft. “It’s me.”

“Solar,” said the one on the right, voice just as soft. “No—really. It’s me.”

Yunjin muttered, “This is the part where I say something unhelpful like ‘follow your heart’ and you hit me.”

Solar moved closer. The mirrors around their ankles reflected an infinite cherry orchard of Cherrys and Cherries, a dizzying multiplication. Neat trick: if Solar looked at their hands, one pair wore Minji’s woven bracelet. But the other pair wore it too. If Solar watched for how one tucked a stray hair—both did.

“You can’t logic this,” Yunjin murmured. “This room eats logic. Try something else.”

Solar breathed in. The gold thread hummed against her pulse. The paper crane warmed her pocket.

“Okay,” she said, voice barely above a whisper. “If you’re Minji, you’ll remember this.”

Both Cherrys leaned forward.

“That day in the hallway,” Solar said. “When you pressed the paper crane into my hand. What did you say—not out loud, but in your head.”

The world held its breath.

Left-Cherry smiled. “I hoped you’d keep it.”

Right-Cherry smiled too—but her eyes went glassy, like someone remembering a line from a script. “I hoped you’d keep it,” she echoed.

Solar’s heart thudded. Close. But the cadence was wrong. The pause in the sentence was wrong.

She turned to the left. “You didn’t just hope,” Solar said. “You swore. To yourself. That if I kept it, you’d stop hiding.”

Left-Cherry flinched, a tiny, truth-shaped movement.

Right-Cherry flinched too—but a beat behind, like lag.

Solar lifted her hand slowly and placed her palm—sigil first—against Left-Cherry’s forehead. Warmth flooded her. A second shard of light prickled at her skin, then sank into her chest.

[System] Shard reclaimed: 2/3.

Right-Cherry blurred, image tearing like cheap fabric. Beneath the friendly surface, something leaner and meaner stared out, eyes narrow.

“You’re good at this,” the not-Cherry sneered. “Careful. The Sanctum doesn’t forgive cleanly.”

The facsimile peeled away and fled into the alleyways, leaving only the real Cherry—Minji-souled—standing, swaying. Yunjin caught her.

Solar’s throat ached. She touched Cherry’s shoulder, light as a leaf. “You okay?”

Cherry nodded, voice small but steady. “It’s strange. Like I’m standing too close to the speaker. But I can feel myself…locking in. Piece by piece.”

“Then one more.” Solar closed her eyes for a beat. “One more and we take you home.”

Cherry met her gaze—Minji peering through the familiar, dear face of her closest friend. Something in Solar’s chest tangled and untangled all at once.

“Go,” Minji-Cherry whispered. “I’ll follow.”

The streets shivered. A spire rose at the city’s heart, made of stacked nameplates and hospital bracelets and paper wristbands from concerts. Names upon names, some crossed out, some doubled. At the spire’s top: a bell that looked like it had never been rung.

[System] Destination unlocked: The Sanctum of Names.

Yunjin rolled her shoulders like she did before going on stage. “Finale time.”

5) The Sanctum of Names

The Sanctum was a room and a cathedral and a throat. Pillars of etched glass rose around them, every surface carved with letters: nicknames, stage names, given names, mistakes, slurs, pet names whispered into collars. The air vibrated with the weight of being called and miscalled, found and lost.

At the center: Minji’s body, sleeping on a platform of woven light. She looked perfect and too still. No bruise. No breath.

Solar’s hands shook. She stepped forward and felt the floor test her weight as if deciding whether she deserved it.

A shape uncurled from behind the platform: not the warden from the river, but a new keeper. They had no face at all, only a blank oval where one should be, and a voice that sounded like a dozen people speaking in harmony.

“Chosen,” it said. “We guard the crossing. Souls that slide, bodies that wait. To restore, you must pay.”

“I know,” Solar whispered. “Name the cost.”

The keeper spread blank hands. “You carry three defenses. Give one. And speak the true name of the one you claim, without flinching.”

Yunjin shot Solar a look. “I can’t help with names. But the defense thing? I vote we stop doing the ‘I’m fine’ shtick. No offense.”

“None taken,” Solar said, smiling faintly through tears.

She knew what the defenses were. They stacked like thin glass: the joke-that-escapes, the mask-that-smiles, the posture of almost—the posture that let her dance near the thing she wanted and call it safety when it was really scared.

She held out her hands. “Take the mask,” she said quietly. “The one that smiles while I bleed.”

Something tugged free from her chest, light and ache at once. She hissed. Yunjin’s hand was there, warm and grounding.

The keeper accepted the invisible offering. “And now,” it said, “Say her name. Not the public one. Not the performance. The one that lives in your mouth when you’re alone.”

Solar swallowed. Her heart hammered. Words felt like a cliff.

She stepped closer to Minji’s sleeping body and leaned in—as if confiding. The sigil on her palm warmed until it was almost unbearable, the gold thread tightening gently around her wrist like a reminder not to run.

“Minji,” Solar breathed. Not staged. Not sharp. Soft and low, the way she would say it in a kitchen in the morning, or into a pillow in the middle of the night, or into a doorway after rehearsal when she should have said it sooner. She said it the way she meant it.

The air trembled. The letters carved into the pillars rearranged, sliding into a single shining path that connected Solar’s wrist to Minji’s sternum.

“Again,” the keeper said.

She said it again—steadier, braver. The bell above them quivered, then tolled once, a note like light striking metal.

[System] Final shard present. Commence tether.

A third flicker of light rose from the floor, skittered around like a moth, then darted to Solar and nested just beneath her collarbone. Her ribs expanded around it.

“Wait,” Yunjin said softly. “If the third shard is in you, how does it get—”

Solar already knew. The cost wasn’t just surrendering a defense; it was being the bridge. The path ran through her.

“Don’t worry,” Solar said, and if her voice shook, it still held. “I won’t be weird.”

Yunjin’s eyes warmed. “You better not.”

Solar pressed her palm to Minji’s chest. Heat surged: not like fire, but like standing in the doorway of a sunlit room after being cold for too long. The gold thread along her wrist flowed out, unfurling into a lattice of light that sank into skin. Her own breath hitched, synced to a rhythm beneath her hand that wasn’t there and then was.

“Come home,” Solar whispered.

The world thinned. The keeper stepped back. The letters in the pillars rose in a sparkling updraft and then fell, like confetti.

For a heartbeat, nothing.

Then—movement. The smallest, shyest inhale. Minji’s lashes fluttered.

Light tore through Solar’s chest like a comet arcing from her to Minji. It burned in the best way: a bright ache that said this matters. It left her not empty but raw, the way skin feels after you pull off a bandage and realize air can kiss you.

Minji’s eyes opened.

They were Minji’s. Not Cherry’s. Not anyone’s but hers.

She looked confused for a second, then found Solar’s face like it was a lighthouse she’d been steering toward her whole life.

“Hi,” Minji whispered, voice cracked and gorgeous. “You came.”

Solar laughed, breath hitching somewhere between sob and relief. “Of course I did.”

The keeper inclined its blank head. “Debt paid. Crossing sealed.”

The Sanctum sighed. The pillars softened back into ordinary glass. Somewhere far away—but also here—the sound of a hospital monitor faded, replaced by the ordinary hum of electricity and the static of city night. Gravity remembered how to hold them.

Minji blinked again, dazed. “Cherry—where is—”

“I’m here,” said Cherry from the doorway, her own soul settling in behind her eyes like someone slipping into their own bed after a long trip. She looked… herself. Whole. She exhaled and gave Solar a watery smile. “I think I hate magic.”

“Same,” Yunjin said, swiping at her face. “Ten out of ten, no notes, never again.”

Solar’s knees finally gave out, and she sank to the floor beside the platform. Minji reached for her without thinking, hand landing at Solar’s nape, thumb finding the place that made Solar feel seen and slightly ruined.

“I have so many questions,” Minji said softly, wonder curling the edges of her voice. “But… later?”

“Later,” Solar echoed. The word tasted like a promise now, not a dodge.

Above them, for the first time, the bell didn’t ring. It didn’t need to. The sound was already inside them, quiet and ongoing, like breath.

[System] Quest complete: Return Minji to her body.
 Rewards: +Clarity, +Courage, +One Mask Less.
 New quest (optional): Live.

The HUD text faded. The world settled back into the ordinary hall’s hush, the fluorescents buzzing like nothing wild had happened at all.

But Solar’s palm still held the faint gold curve of the sigil, a tender warmth under her skin that said: you did it.
 And Minji—Minji was here, eyes bright, breath real, her name fitting perfectly in Solar’s mouth.

Outside the window, somewhere, a siren wailed and a bus sighed to a stop and a dog barked and life continued, uncomplicated in its complication.

“Hey,” Yunjin said lightly, voice thick. “Main character. You gonna sit there forever, or are you going to help me get our girl some water?”

Solar stood, legs shaky, heart steady. “Yeah,” she said, smiling at Minji, then Cherry, then Yunjin. “Let’s go home.”




Act V — The Return

The world didn’t snap back all at once. It unraveled slowly, like threads slipping through Solar’s fingers. The humming Sanctum faded first, the carved names melting into plain, scuffed tile. Then the golden lattice of light dissolved into the air, leaving only a faint warmth beneath Solar’s skin.

And then there was Minji.

Her breathing was shallow but real, her eyes open and darting as if to confirm she was truly here. Her fingers flexed experimentally against Solar’s wrist, clumsy but alive. It was too much, too fast, and yet not enough—Solar wanted to hold her tighter, but she was afraid Minji might crumble, like glass still cooling from fire.

Yunjin broke the silence first. “Well, damn,” she said, sniffling and pretending she wasn’t. “Guess you’re a healer now. Solar of Le Sserafim, Savior of Souls, Slayer of River Goblins. Put that in your bio.”

Solar huffed a laugh, voice shaking. “Shut up.”

Cherry stumbled forward, bracing herself against the wall. She looked pale but steady now, like her body had finally remembered how to feel like hers again. “I don’t ever want to be two people again,” she muttered. “Zero out of ten. Would not recommend.”

“Girl, you were iconic,” Yunjin said, grinning at her through tears. “Double-Cherry. Buy one, get one free.”

Cherry gave her the finger, but it lacked venom. “I just want… my bed.”

Solar’s heart ached with affection for both of them. Yunjin with her ridiculous bravado, Cherry with her deadpan exhaustion. But her eyes kept returning to Minji, who was struggling to sit up.

Solar reached instinctively, sliding an arm behind her back to steady her. “Easy. Don’t push yourself.”

Minji leaned against her, head falling lightly to Solar’s shoulder. “It feels… strange,” she murmured. Her voice wasn’t Cherry’s anymore. It wasn’t split or glitched. It was Minji’s—low, careful, threaded with vulnerability. “Like being gone but not really gone. Like a dream I almost didn’t wake from.”

Solar swallowed, eyes burning. “You’re here now. That’s what matters.”

Minji’s lips curved into a small, wobbly smile. “Because of you.”

Solar froze. The words were too heavy, too sharp, and yet they slid straight into her chest like they belonged there. She wanted to protest, to deflect, to laugh it off—but the mask was gone. She’d traded it away.

So she just whispered, “Always.”


The Walk Back

They left the building together, the fluorescent hum fading into the night air. It was cold outside, damp with the lingering memory of rain. The city looked almost normal again—traffic lights blinking, a bus roaring down the avenue, neon signs buzzing lazily. But Solar could still see the edges glitching faintly, like the world hadn’t fully decided to be ordinary yet.

Yunjin shoved her hands in her pockets, leading the way with exaggerated swagger. “You know, not to be dramatic, but I think we just went through, like, four JRPG expansions in one night. Where’s my XP bar?”

Cherry groaned. “You get negative XP for singing buffs that sounded like goat noises.”

Solar almost laughed, but Minji’s hand brushed hers—barely a touch, but enough to root her in place. She glanced sideways. Minji was watching the ground, hair falling over her eyes, but her pinky lingered close to Solar’s like an invitation.

Solar’s chest tightened. She wanted to take it. God, she wanted to. But instead she curled her fist in her pocket, afraid of what it would mean.

“You okay?” she asked softly.

Minji nodded once, then shook her head. “Not really. But… I will be.” She looked up at Solar then, and her gaze was so clear, so Minji, that Solar forgot how to breathe for a second. “Thanks to you.”


The Quiet Room

Back at the dorm, the others collapsed instantly. Yunjin sprawled across the couch with a dramatic sigh. Cherry faceplanted on her bed without removing her shoes. The building was silent except for the occasional hum of the heater and the rustle of blankets.

Solar sat on the edge of her own bed, heart still racing. She stared at her palm, at the faint gold crescent still glowing under her skin. Proof. Evidence. A reminder.

A soft knock startled her. Minji stood in the doorway, sleeves of her hoodie falling over her hands. Her face was pale, tired, but she looked more herself than Solar had ever seen her.

“Can I come in?”

Solar nodded wordlessly. Minji slipped inside and sat beside her, close enough that their shoulders touched.

They didn’t speak at first. The silence stretched, heavy but not uncomfortable. Then Minji exhaled. “I thought I was going to disappear in there,” she said quietly. “That I’d never feel like me again.”

Solar turned to her, throat tight. “You almost did. I almost lost you.”

Minji’s eyes softened. “But you didn’t.” She tilted her head, studying Solar like she was memorizing her face. “You fought for me.”

Solar tried to joke, tried to make it lighter, but the words came out raw: “Of course I did. I couldn’t… I couldn’t let you go.”

The air between them thickened, heavy with everything unsaid. Minji’s lips parted, like she was about to respond—but instead she reached out, fingers brushing Solar’s wrist where the faint gold thread pulsed.

“It’s still there,” Minji whispered. “You’re still carrying me.”

Solar’s chest cracked open. She wanted to say she didn’t mind, that she’d carry Minji forever if it meant she stayed safe. But the words stuck, too big, too bare.

Instead, she whispered, “You’re safe now. That’s all I need.”

Minji’s eyes lingered on her, searching. Then, slowly, she leaned forward and rested her forehead against Solar’s shoulder. Just that. Nothing more.

Solar let out a shaky breath and closed her eyes. For the first time since the crash, since the river, since the endless corridors of “almost,” she felt still.



Act VI — The Aftermath

1) Morning, With Gravity

Solar wakes before the alarm. For a moment she’s sure the room will still be a cathedral of glass, a ribcage of bells and names — but it’s just her ceiling, hair tickling her cheek, and Minji’s slow breath warming the crook of Solar’s shoulder.

They didn’t mean to fall asleep like that. They did anyway.

The gold crescent in Solar’s palm glows so faintly it could be imagined. When she shifts, Minji makes a small sound and tightens her arm around Solar’s waist like a reflex. It rips a soft sound out of Solar too — not pain, exactly. More like the ache after a knot has finally been worked out.

No mask, the Sanctum took that. So when Minji’s eyes flutter open, Solar doesn’t hide the relief that crashes through her face. She lets it happen. Lets Minji see it.

“Morning,” Minji whispers, voice hoarse with sleep. She looks at Solar the way you look at a window on a plane and see the curve of the earth. A little stunned. A little reverent.

“Hey,” Solar says, and it sounds like the version of herself she is when no one’s watching. “How’s your… everything?”

Minji blinks slow. “My body fits again.” She tests her fingers, flexing them. “The rest is catching up.”

Solar nods. “Okay. We go slow.”

Minji’s mouth tips. “We already did the part where we die and come back. ‘Slow’ sounds radical.”

They lie there for a while, the room loud with small things — the heater coughing, someone in another unit laughing at their phone, Cherry snoring on the other side of the wall like an offended cat. When they finally sit up, Solar’s palm hums once, a gentle bell-tone under the skin. Minji’s gaze flicks to it and then to Solar’s face.

“It didn’t go away,” Minji says softly.

Solar rubs the mark with her thumb. “Feels like it’s… not for show. Like a scar. But nice?”

“Pretty scar,” Minji decides, and slides her fingers across Solar’s palm like she’s smoothing a crease. The glow softens, a content animal settling down.

There’s a knock. Then Yunjin’s voice through the door, as subtle as a foghorn: “Rise and hydrate, heroes. I made coffee and a morally confusing number of waffles.”

“Coming,” Solar calls, and Minji smiles at the plural before she can stop herself.

2) Cherry, Rebooted

Cherry is already at the table, hair a mess, wearing a sweatshirt that claims she ran a 10K in 2019 (she did not). She’s staring at her Dutch Bros cup like it owes her money. “I have decided,” she announces, “to never let a soul rent my body again. Not even for a weekend.”

Yunjin slides a plate down in front of her. “Hard but fair boundary.”

Cherry pokes a waffle with her fork. “Do I… feel different to anyone?” She looks at Solar, then Minji. Her eyes are her own — dry humor, knife-bright care — but there’s a shadow, the kind that appears the day after you cry even if no one saw you do it.

“You feel like Cherry,” Solar says, because it’s true. “Just… closer to the surface.”

Cherry’s mouth lifts. “Weird compliment. I’ll take it.”

Minji sits gingerly, as if the chair might reject her. Yunjin immediately plops a mug in front of her and points two fingers at Minji’s eyes like she’s a coach. “Hydrate, carb-load, then bare minimum one nap. Day plan set.”

“Bossy,” Minji murmurs, almost smiling.

“Mother,” Yunjin corrects, hand over chest. “And yes.”

Solar makes a plate and pretends her hands aren’t shaking. Minji sees anyway. She reaches under the table and just… rests her foot against Solar’s sneaker. It’s the smallest touch. It is also an anchor.

Cherry takes a bite, chews, swallows, then squints at Minji. “So. You remember being me?”

Minji considers. “It was like wearing your favorite hoodie, but it’s not yours. Comfortable. Wrong. I kept reaching for the pocket and it wasn’t where I remembered.”

Cherry nods, gaze going someplace private. “And me watching you use my hands felt like… hearing my voice on a recording I didn’t know about. Familiar and too loud.” She huffs. “Weirdest sleepover ever.”

No one laughs. They don’t need to. The quiet is honest and isn’t trying to fix anything. Solar’s chest loosens around that: honesty, unadorned.

3) Rules for a Soft Day (Yunjin’s Patch Notes)

After the second round of waffles (illegal), Yunjin claps like a PE teacher. “Okay, patch notes for today: We stay inside. We do not over-process. We do three Nice Things, no more, no less. I propose: hot showers, fresh sheets, and a Studio Ghibli movie. Also everybody picks one truth to say out loud so the River of Messy Feelings doesn’t sneak back through the vents.”

Cherry raises a hand. “Can my truth be that I hate Howl because he is dramatic?”

“Absolutely not,” Yunjin says, scandalized. “We respect messy men who do their own hair.”

Minji glances at Solar, a question tucked in the tilt of her head. Solar shrugs, letting the corner of her mouth tip up. “I can do three nice things.”

“Good.” Yunjin points at Solar’s palm. “And if your magic tattoo starts glowing like a rave, you tell me immediately.”

“It’s not—” Solar starts, then stops. She looks at the crescent. “Okay. I will.”

They disperse. Nice Thing #1: Showers. Steam curls through the hall. Solar stands under hot water until her skin hums, fingers pressed over the ghost-warmth on her wrist, remembering the feel of Minji’s name leaving her mouth the way you remember a pain that healed — sharp, then gone, then something softer.

When she steps out, a folded towel sits on the sink with a note in Minji’s handwriting (she recognizes it instantly): Stay warm. — M

Solar’s breath stutters. She presses the towel to her face and laughs into it, stupidly happy.

Nice Thing #2: Fresh sheets. Yunjin and Cherry stage a laundry takeover, moving like a two-person pit crew. Minji helps Solar wrestle corners over the mattress. Their fingers brush, and the room does that thing where it tilts an inch and then rights itself. The crescent under Solar’s skin lights once, dim and kind. Minji glances up, lips parted. Neither of them says it. Both of them know.

Nice Thing #3: Movie nest. The living room becomes a field of blankets. Cherry, recovered enough to reclaim sarcasm at full power, insists on “Princess Mononoke because it’s about souls and wolves and also because sometimes you have to fight a forest god.” Yunjin cries at exactly the parts she always cries at. Minji leans into Solar’s shoulder, and Solar doesn’t move away.

One truth each, Yunjin decreed. They keep forgetting — on purpose, maybe — until the credits roll and the room goes blue with the streaming app’s idle screen. It’s Minji who breaks the stalemate.

“My truth,” she says quietly, eyes on her hands. “Is that when I was… displaced, I wasn’t afraid of not coming back as much as I was afraid of what would be different if I did.” She swallows. “I kept thinking: what if I came back and I didn’t fit anywhere. Even here.” She gestures vaguely — the room, the nest, Solar’s shoulder. “With you.”

No mask, Solar promised herself. She lets the ache show. “My truth is that I’ve been living in ‘almost’ because it felt like control. And I’m… tired. I want a thing that is not pretending to be something else.” She looks at Minji long enough that the words can’t be mistaken. “With you.”

Cherry clears her throat. When they look over, she’s staring at the TV with the ferocity of someone being polite on purpose. “My truth is that I was jealous for five minutes but it wasn’t about either of you. It was about losing the illusion that I’m the safest person in the room because no one can reach me.” She exhales, shoulders dropping. “Turns out I can be reached. I hate it. It’s probably healthy.”

Yunjin sniffs. “My truth is I’m proud of us,” she says, voice wobbling. “And if anyone says something cursed about destiny or star-crossed souls, I’ll start singing debuff songs until the glass breaks.”

“Please don’t,” Cherry says. “We just washed the sheets.”

Laughter lands and stays. It doesn’t fix anything. It doesn’t need to.

4) Echoes & Gifts

Afternoon thins into the kind of gray that turns windows into mirrors. Solar wanders to the kitchen for water and finds, tucked beside the kettle, a small yellow paper crane — the one from the river, she realizes — neat, warm to the touch. She opens it carefully. Inside, ink she didn’t put there:

A name spoken twice opens a door.
 — A.

“The Archivist?” Solar whispers, glancing over her shoulder like she’ll find a librarian made of ticket stubs perched on the counter. Nothing. Just the faint citrus smell of dish soap and the sound of Minji laughing at something Yunjin said.

She refolds the crane and pockets it. Not ominous. Just… a rule. A gift, maybe. Or a warning.

Her phone, forgotten for the entire saga, buzzes on the counter. Notifications avalanche: missed calls, a text from a manager about the accident, a short “are you safe?” from a number saved as Crusty (Solar startles; even in dreams he’s responsible), and one from Unknown that just says: Glad you made it through 🙂

Solar stares. The smiley face is wrong. Friendly like a sticker slapped over a bruise.

She locks the screen. Not today. The world can be big again tomorrow.

When she turns, Minji is there, one hand still tangled in the blanket she dragged with her, eyes soft with questions she’s not asking yet. “You good?”

“I am,” Solar says — and means it. “I found a note. From the Archivist, I think.”

“What’s it say?”

Solar opens her mouth. Then remembers the line: A name spoken twice opens a door. She thinks of bells. Of thresholds. Of the way Minji’s name sounded in the Sanctum when she said it like a promise.

“Later,” Solar says, and Minji nods. Later, here, doesn’t feel like a dodge; it feels like a time they’ll actually get to.

5) The First Door

Dusk bruises into night. Cherry dozes. Yunjin attempts dinner and invents a new genre of edible. Minji ends up at the tiny table with Solar, sleeves shoved up, hair messy in the way that undoes Solar’s breathing.

“I don’t want to rush any of this,” Minji says suddenly, like she’s been negotiating with herself all evening. “I don’t even know what day it is. But I also don’t want to pretend the river didn’t happen. Or the bell.” She laces her fingers together and then, tremulous, unlatches them again to lay her hand palm-up on the table. A small, brave offer.

Solar sets her hand in Minji’s. Not tight. Not claiming. Knowing. The crescent under Solar’s skin warms — not a flare, more a hum that says yes, here.

Minji exhales, like that hum passed through her too. “Can I try something?” she asks, eyes flicking to Solar’s mouth and back so fast it could be excused as a mistake.

Solar’s pulse hammers. “Okay.”

Minji squeezes once, a question. Solar squeezes back, answer. Then Minji says Solar’s name once, very quietly, the way you test a key in a lock.

It doesn’t open anything. It lands and waits.

Minji says it again.

The ceiling light flickers like a glitch. The room does that threshold tilt, a bell tone faint as the inside of a seashell. For a breath the air smells like the Sanctum — not antiseptic, not dust, but that particular clean you get after rain. A door opens, but it’s not a physical door. It’s a permission.

Solar leans forward, and Minji does too, and their foreheads touch before anything else. Not a kiss. A begin.

“I don’t want almost,” Minji says, so soft Solar has to feel it more than hear it.

“Me neither,” Solar whispers back, and everything inside her that used to insist on detours falls quiet.

They sit like that until Yunjin yells from the kitchen, “Dinner’s emotionally ready!” and Cherry deadpans, without opening her eyes, “Put your forehead romance on pause.”

They don’t move for one more heartbeat. Then they go.

6) Notifications

They eat at the couch again because the table has become sacred ground. Cherry, revived by food, gives a dramatic retelling of watching herself fold laundry from the perspective of having not done it and takes a bow when Yunjin throws a sock at her.

Solar’s phone buzzes again. She ignores it until Minji nudges her knee. “Check it. If it’s bad, I’ll delete it with my mind.”

Solar snorts, digs the phone out. A slew of normal messages — friends, staff, her brother dropping a single unhinged sticker — and one more from Unknown:

Pretty scar.
 Pretty tether.
 Pretty you. Let’s play again soon.

Solar’s skin goes cold. She looks up at Minji, who’s watching her face instead of the screen. “What is it?” Minji asks, voice dipping low, alert.

Solar flips the phone so Minji can read. Yunjin catches the shift in the air immediately and leans over the back of the couch. Cherry sits up.

“Block,” Cherry says, too fast.

“Save screenshots,” Yunjin counters. “We’re not in the river anymore. We build a paper trail.”

Minji’s hand finds Solar’s wrist under the blanket, thumb pressing once, twice, like the sigil’s pulse. “Not today,” she says. “We’ll handle it. But not today.”

Solar nods. The fear is real; the warmth is louder. She screenshots, forwards to Yunjin (who names the contact Ominous Clown), then blocks the number. When she locks the screen, she looks at Minji and says her name once in her head — the kitchen way, the pillow way — and once out loud, barely a breath.

The room shifts — just a fraction. Enough to feel the door open if they need it.

They don’t. Not yet. But it’s there.

7) Sleep, With Bells

They drift to bed in a way that feels earned. Cherry flips them all off tenderly and threatens to call at 6 a.m. to make sure no one died of feelings. Yunjin hugs Solar tighter than usual and whispers, “You did good,” in the kind of voice Solar knows Yunjin saves for the wings before a stage.

In the quiet dark, Minji stands in Solar’s doorway and hesitates. “Can I—” she starts, then aborts. “No, that’s presumptuous. I just… after being nowhere, being near helps.”

Solar lifts the blanket without comment. Minji slides in, careful with elbows and breath. They face each other, inches of air and a bell tone between their mouths. No kiss. Not yet. The not-yet isn’t a punishment anymore. It’s a path.

“Say it,” Minji whispers. Solar doesn’t ask what. She says Minji’s name twice, softer than before. The door opens again inside the air. They step through it by doing nothing but staying.

“Later,” Minji says, smiling drowsy. “For real this time.”

“For real,” Solar says, and the words hook into something solid.

The crescent on Solar’s palm cools to a warmth that feels indistinguishable from a heartbeat she’s beginning to recognize as shared. Somewhere, the Archivist closes a file with satisfaction. Somewhere else, a warden made of jealous glances hisses and recedes.

And here: two girls, one bed, a future that isn’t an orb stolen from a river but a thing they will build, awake.

Solar sleeps. When she dreams, it’s ordinary: a kettle boiling, a folded note, Minji laughing in a kitchen with windows thrown open to morning.

When she wakes, the light will be real.




Act VII — The World Knocks

1) Push Notifications

Morning slides in without asking permission. The room tastes like leftover sleep and warm cotton. Solar’s phone vibrates against the nightstand: three texts in quick succession, then a voicemail, then the dull thunder of a group chat resurrecting itself after catastrophe.

Minji’s eyes open where she’s tucked in the other half of the pillow. She blinks, still that small bit dazed, then huffs a puff of hair off her lips. “Your phone is committing war crimes.”

Solar groans, flops an arm across her face, and fishes for the device. The lock screen is a confession of everything that exists outside this room:
 — Manager: Update me when possible. Car incident reported. Are you safe?
 — Crusty: Saw something about a wreck. You good? Ping when you can.
 — Jaehyun: (sticker of a barricade and then another of a cat flexing biceps)
 — Unknown: Miss me?

That last one curdles the air. Solar flips the phone facedown.

Minji watches her, reading the flinch like sheet music. “Later,” she says, voice firm in a new way. “We choose later.”

Solar nods. She doesn’t say thank you. She reaches out and tucks a flyaway behind Minji’s ear. The gesture lands like a stone in a lake—ripples, then calm.

Yunjin thumps the door with her heel and hollers, “News check-in in ten! PR mom has clocked in! Coffee will be medicinal!”

“PR mom,” Minji repeats, smiling tiny. “We should go face her wrath.”

“Wrath = waffles,” Solar says. “You’ll live.”

2) Triage

The living room’s become a war room. Yunjin’s hair is in a messy bun that could be classified as a cyclone warning. Cherry’s at the whiteboard (when did they get a whiteboard?), drawing a flowchart that reads: ACCIDENT → SPECULATION → WE SAY LITTLE → TOUCH GRASS. A post-it nearby: DRINK WATER U FREAKS.

“Okay,” Yunjin says, clapping once. “Everybody take a breath. Here’s the state of the union. There’s a blurry pic of the wreck, no injuries reported, and one fan account saying they saw me at a convenience store nearby with a ‘mystery girl in a hoodie.’” She points at Minji’s borrowed hoodie. “That hoodie.”

Cherry twirls the whiteboard marker. “Mystery girl identified as ‘probably Solar because fate is a hack.’”

Solar fights an unhelpful laugh. Minji presses her lips together to keep one in.

Yunjin continues, commander-calm. “We don’t feed the beast. Solar texts the manager I am safe; not ready to share details; will circle back. Minji, you don’t respond to anything. Cherry, you stop doomscrolling.” Cherry guiltily pockets her phone. “And me? I will politely harass the rumor mill by posting waffles and an aggressively normal caption.”

Minji slides onto the couch like you do when you’re not sure you’re allowed to sit. “What… should I be? I mean— how public should…” She falters.

“What you are right now is a person in sweats who survived a thing,” Yunjin says gently. “We can name anything else later.”

Solar types the manager text with both of them watching; it’s like balancing on a beam. She hits send. Her phone hums again immediately: Unknown with a single emoji—🎭. She screenshots, forwards to Yunjin, blocks, and then inhales like surfacing.

Cherry taps the board. “Next line item: we do one normal errand so no one combusts. Groceries? Sunlight? Both?”

“Sunlight,” Minji says too quickly, then softer: “Please.”

3) A DM in the Kitchen

They gear up: masks, hats, the armor of mundanity. Solar heads to the kitchen for water and finds Cherry leaning against the counter, stare pinned to her phone like it owes rent.

“You too?” Solar asks.

Cherry flips the screen around. A message request from A Friend: The river hasn’t forgotten you. It never does. Keep your doors locked. There’s a link. Cherry hasn’t clicked it.

Solar’s hand finds Cherry’s wrist before she thinks about it. The crescent under her skin warms, and Cherry exhale-laughs, something like relief. “I’m fine,” Cherry says. “I’m just…not used to being porous.”

“You’re allowed to be reachable and safe,” Solar says, feeling the truth settle in her mouth on the way out. “We’ll report it. And then we’ll go buy spinach we won’t eat.”

Cherry smirks. “Promise me we’ll forget it in the crisper, like God intended.”

“Scout’s honor.”

Yunjin sweeps in, confiscates the phone like a benevolent bouncer. “Screenshots, report, block,” she narrates, thumbs flashing. “I will be a wall; you can be a person.” She kisses Cherry’s hair without ceremony. Cherry pretends to hate it and leans in a fraction.

4) Errands, Thresholds

They walk. The morning is bright-shy, the kind of blue that makes edges look cut out and pasted back on. Their corner store smells like neon and microwaved buns. They buy juice, fruit, too many snacks. Minji drifts down the tea aisle like a ghost learning the price of oolong.

At the register, the clerk glances between them and says, tone perfectly neutral, “You girls okay?”

“Yes,” Yunjin says at the exact moment Cherry says, “Define okay,” and Solar says, “We’re getting there,” and Minji says nothing, just nods once and meets the clerk’s eyes, and for whatever reason, that feels like an answer.

Outside, Minji stops under the crooked awning. “Can we… not go back yet? Just sit?” Her voice is steady now, but her fingers worry the edge of the paper bag like a nervous habit she wasn’t aware of having.

They sit on the low concrete planter where ivy is attempting a revolution. Traffic breathes. A dog drags its human past and wags at Yunjin like they’re old friends. Cherry feeds a sparrow a single, illegal cracker crumb.

Solar’s knee bumps Minji’s. They don’t apologize.

“Tell me something true,” Minji says, eyes on the ivy. “Something you haven’t said out loud.”

Solar lets the world pass once, twice. Then: “When I said your name in that place, it felt like stepping into a room I’ve wanted to live in for a long time and realizing it already had my things in it.”

Minji’s breath catches. She looks up so fast a curl falls over her brow. “Say it again,” she says, too quickly to be casual, then gentler: “Please.”

Solar swallows. “Minji.”

The air tilts. A faint bell, barely there, like the memory of metal. Minji’s mouth shapes a thank you she doesn’t say.

“Your turn,” Solar says.

Minji thinking is quiet. You can see it in how she blinks, how she flexes her fingers like she’s counting with her bones. “When I was… misplaced,” she says, choosing the word like she’s picking glass out of a cut, “the only thing that stayed the same was the way your name sounded in my head. I kept it like a lifeline.” She looks at Solar, eyes wide-open. “Solar.”

It isn’t the name the keeper requested. It’s the one the world uses. But Minji says it like the kitchen version, the pillow version—soft edges, no stage. The bell tone hums, truer. A door: not physical, but the same door as last night, opening between them like an option.

Cherry clears her throat pointedly and gestures with her chin toward the end of the block. A girl with a camera pauses, hesitates, pretends to tie her shoe. Yunjin has clocked her, too. She stands without drama, stretching like a cat, and turns her body so the sun flares the lens.

“Home?” Yunjin suggests. “We’ve touched grass. Grass has been touched.”

Minji nods, but her hand brushes Solar’s; fingertips catch. It feels like asking. Solar lets their hands slip apart, not away. The door hums all the way back.

5) Kitchen Light

Back inside, the apartment smells faintly like detergent and Yunjin’s chaotic dinner prep from last night. Cherry announces nap o’clock and vanishes. Yunjin vanishes with her, muttering something about “just in case a phone tries to fight us.”

Solar and Minji end up in the kitchen because gravity said so. The kettle is a relic of ordinary days. Solar fills it. Minji leans against the counter and watches like the act might teach her a language.

“Do we… talk about last night?” Solar asks, not looking away from the water level. “Or do we let it be what it was and build from the ground we’re standing on?”

Minji considers. “Yes,” she says, which is ridiculous and exactly right. “We name the parts that need naming. We don’t make a religion out of a miracle.”

Solar laughs, surprised. “Are you always this wise, or is this a post-Sanctum patch?”

“Limited-time event,” Minji deadpans, and they both grin into the steam like teenagers, which they are not, and also are.

Silence folds back in. Not tense. Not empty. Expectant.

Minji’s mouth curves. She lifts two fingers, barely touches Solar’s wrist, right where the crescent rests. “Solar,” she whispers.

The light changes temperature. Solar could swear the tile shrugs into a better alignment.

Minji tilts her head, closer now. “Solar,” she says again.

Between them, something quiet and unquestioning opens. It isn’t fireworks. It isn’t a choir. It’s a hallway lit by a lamp left on for you because someone knew you were coming home late.

Solar meets her halfway.

The first kiss lands like an exhale you didn’t know you’d been holding since you learned how to breathe. Not urgent. Not performative. Warm. Present. The faintest tremor in Minji’s mouth, which Solar answers by staying. Time doesn’t disappear; it just stops yelling.

Minji’s hand finds the hem of Solar’s tee, settles there, asking nothing, grounding everything. Solar slides her palm up to Minji’s jaw, thumb brushing the place where a bell tone lives under skin now. The crescent hums, a purr, then settles as if to say: noted.

They part because air is a thing. Minji lowers her forehead to Solar’s, breath laugh-light. “Hi,” she says, as if they haven’t been saying it in every other language all morning.

“Hi,” Solar echoes, giddy enough to be stupid about it.

In the hall, something drops—Yunjin, probably, losing a war with a laundry basket—and both of them jump, then press their mouths together again because the world isn’t going to stop knocking, and this is what you do: you build a room inside the noise.

6) Postscript, Practical

They make tea and actually drink it. The group chat pings; Yunjin handles it. Cherry wakes up long enough to declare, “You’re glowing, I’m blind, congrats, shut up,” and goes back under. Solar texts Manager: Safe. Need a day. Will debrief tomorrow. Crusty gets: All good. Will scrim later? The reply is a thumbs-up and a long line of keyboard smash that translates to relieved.

Solar opens the paper crane again while Minji watches the kettle steam like it’s the only TV she’ll allow. A name spoken twice opens a door. On the back, in tiny script she missed before: Doors open both ways. Choose which ones to close.

She shows Minji. They look at it, twin creases between their brows. “We’ll figure it out,” Minji says, not as a vow but as a work plan. Solar loves her a little violently for that.

The blocked number stays blocked. The screenshots live in a folder labeled evidence. The future is not a river orb stolen early; it’s a grocery list and a text to HR and rehearsal schedules and the knowledge that in a kitchen lit by 11 a.m., they said each other’s names and a door opened.

7) The Knock

Evening comes willing. They eat something edible. Cherry doesn’t float; Yunjin doesn’t sing; the house feels like a house. The door knocks once.

Three heads swivel. Yunjin moves first, checks the peephole, relaxes, and opens.

On the mat: a small, neat package. No return label. Inside, wrapped in tissue, a bell clapper—the inner piece of a bell, smooth from use, cold from absence. A note, typed:

You rang. See you soon.
 — D.

Silence rearranges itself. Cherry says, “I hate Amazon,” because humor is a raft. Yunjin takes a photo for the paper trail, then sets the clapper down like it might bite. Minji steps behind Solar, not hiding—placing herself where the hum in Solar’s wrist can reach her.

Solar feels fear. She lets it be part of the room, not the architect.

“Doors open both ways,” she says, mostly to herself. She takes Minji’s hand. Minji squeezes, twice.

They don’t answer the note. They don’t touch the clapper again. They double-check the locks, then sit on the floor with their backs to the couch like it’s a fort.

“Tomorrow,” Yunjin says, practical. “We file the report. We loop in adults. We keep living.”

“Tomorrow,” Cherry echoes, and yawns.

Minji turns to Solar, and there’s nothing ceremonial about it now. She kisses Solar once, simple as turning a light on when you walk into a room. “Tonight, we sleep,” she says.

Solar nods. The crescent hums its agreement. Somewhere, a bell without a clapper is silent and still beautiful. Somewhere else, the Archivist files away a card under KISSES (FIRST).

Here, in this kitchen, the door stays open to air and closed to what doesn’t belong. That’s how you do it. That’s how you keep doing it.

And when they turn out the lights and the apartment exhales, the world keeps knocking, but it isn’t the only sound anymore.

6) The Wall

The first thing is the gentle bump of Solar’s back against the corridor wall, not hard, just a boundary saying this is the edge; you are safe inside it. The second is Minji’s hand at Solar’s jaw, not guiding, just holding, like she’s telling the skin there a story. The third is the kiss, which is not cinematic except for the part where it is because all honest things are: warm, careful, with the gravity of decision humming through it.

Solar’s fingers splay against Minji’s waist, touch mapped and unhurried, the kind of holding that says I’m here rather than you’re mine. The crescent warms, agrees, then quiets like a cat kneading and settling. The door in the air stays open; they step into it and realize there’s a lamp on and a rug and a hook for coats they don’t need.

Somewhere, Eunchae squeaks and slaps a hand over her own eyes like a cartoon. Chaewon turns the ladle into a privacy wand. Sakura smiles into her sleeves. Cherry says, “Finally,” sotto voce, and Sunghoon snorts, too pleased with himself.
